Smallest feline in the desert

The Sand Cat (F. margarita), also known as sand dune cat is a small wild cat that inhabits exclusively in desert habitat in Asia and Africa. They have very well adapted to life in the desert that they are able to live without water.

Physical Description


Sand cats are one of the smallest living animals in the desert. The body length of a sand cat is between 40 ~ 52cm. Albeit it has a petite body, the sand cat has a relatively long tail of 20 ~ 30cm. Sand cats weigh only 1.5 ~ 3.5kg, whereas a regular adult domestic cat weighs around 5kg and sometimes more.

Their fur is in a sandy yellow so that they can blend into the desert environment better. Brown streaks also run across their cheeks and the feet, tail area. Their bodies only have faint stripes and around the belly area the fur is whiter. Since they are nocturnal animals, they have large eyes and furry ears that set low on their face to help them to hunt at night, and the fur in the ears act as a sort of protection against occasional sandstorms.

Behaviour

Since sand cats live in the desert, the temperature varied dramatically between day and night, sand cats dig a lot to create burrows to not only keep themselves from the heat in the daytime to prevent water loss as there are very limited water resources in the desert. Due to frequent digging, unlike the regular cats we know, sand cats' claws cannot be fully retracted and are not very sharp.

Sand cats are solitary species, they do not usually gather together unless it is for mating. If kep in captive, they can live up to 17 years with a litter size of average 3 cubs. And to one's surprise, sand cats are not good climbers nor jumpers, they are more of a "down to earth" type of cats. With their exceptional hearing sense, they are able to detect prey, usually rodents, underneath the sand and dig them out. Sand cats also hunt birds, spiders, insects, and reptiles. Sand cats are able to sustain their moisture needs from the prey, which is one of the reasons why they can adapt to desert so well.

Habitat

Sand cats prefer dry, arid habitats with little vegetation mixed with sandy and rocky areas and avoid bare and shifting sand dunes because of minimal prey supplies. They can be found mainly in Africa's Sahara desert, parts of central Asia and Arabian peninsula.

Cat Specialist Group Organization

Main Threats

Although under IUCN, sand cats are under least concern categories. Due to their low population, they are not easy to be sighted. In Abu Dhabi's Al Gharbia area, the sand cats were not sighted from 2003 till 2015, marking an absence of sightings for over 10 years!

The major threat to the sand cat is habitat loss and degradation. Because of human settlements and activities, the arid ecosystems disappear quickly and thus threaten our lovely cute sand cats here. Such as in the Arabian Peninsula, where the sand dune habitat is declining because of political conflicts. Habitat loss can also cause the decline of sand cats' prey, which then indirectly affect the region's livability for sand cats. Additional threats are the introduction to domestic pets, such as dogs and cats, this creates direct competition for prey, predation and disease transmission. The threat from domestic pets is most prominent in Iran, sand cats are often killed by shepherd dogs, or fell for traps meant for other animals.

Fun Facts

  • Make barking noise

    • Contrary to their adorable appearance and name, sand cats do not meow, instead, they make low pitch noises that almost sound like a dog barking. Find it hard to believe? Hear it yourself! International Society for Endangered Cats (ISEC) Canada

  • Ghost walker

    • Sand cats' feet have pads and dense hair that protect them from having direct contact with the extreme heat and cold of their habit. Bear in mind, the temperature in the desert can be above 50 degree Celsius in the day time and drop to 0 degree Celsius at night! Furthermore, the pads provide traction so that they can move swiftly across shifting sands and barely leave any trace behind.

  • Venomous snakes? No problem!

    • Yes, this tiny feline here consumes reptiles that include poisonous vipers and other snakes!

  • Better hearing

    • Despite similar appearance of sand cats' ears and domestic cats' ears. Sand cat's ear canal is roughly twice the size which allows them to have 8 decibels greater hearing sensitivity!

  • Travelers

    • Sand cats are natural travelers, they can move long distances in a single night. On record, one male sand cat in Morocco traveled more than 14 km in a straight line within 30 hours.
